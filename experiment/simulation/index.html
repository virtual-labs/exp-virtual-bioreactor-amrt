Introduction to HRV

What is HRV?

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) refers to the variation in time intervals between consecutive heartbeats. These intervals are called RR intervals (time between two R-peaks in ECG). A healthy heart does not beat at perfectly regular intervals.

RR Intervals

RR intervals are measured in milliseconds between consecutive R-peaks in an ECG signal. Higher variability indicates better adaptability, while lower variability may suggest stress, fatigue, or pathology.

Adaptability Indicator

HRV reflects how well the heart adapts to internal and external stimuli. Higher variability means better adaptability to changing conditions, while lower variability indicates reduced adaptive capacity.

Key Insight

  • High HRV: Better adaptability
  • Low HRV: Stress, fatigue, pathology
  • Normal RR: 600-1200 ms
  • Heart Rate: 50-100 bpm

ECG Signal showing R-peaks and RR intervals — The basis of HRV analysis

The heart is regulated by the Autonomic Nervous System (ANS), which has two branches that work in balance to control heart rate and HRV.

Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS)

"Fight or Flight"
  • Increases heart rate
  • Reduces HRV
  • Activates during stress
  • Exercise response
Low HRV Pattern

Parasympathetic Nervous System (PNS)

"Rest and Digest"
  • Decreases heart rate
  • Increases HRV
  • Active during rest
  • Recovery state
High HRV Pattern

ANS Balance

HRV reflects the dynamic balance between SNS and PNS activity. A healthy individual shows good HRV, indicating proper ANS regulation and adaptability.

HRV Glossary

HRV (Heart Rate Variability)

The variation in time intervals between consecutive heartbeats, reflecting autonomic nervous system activity.

RR Interval

The time between two consecutive R-peaks in an ECG signal, measured in milliseconds.

SDNN

Standard Deviation of NN intervals. A measure of overall HRV reflecting both sympathetic and parasympathetic activity.

RMSSD

Root Mean Square of Successive Differences. Primarily reflects parasympathetic (vagal) activity.

pNN50

Percentage of successive RR intervals that differ by more than 50ms. Another measure of parasympathetic activity.

LF (Low Frequency)

Power in the 0.04-0.15 Hz band. Reflects a mixture of sympathetic and parasympathetic activity.

HF (High Frequency)

Power in the 0.15-0.4 Hz band. Primarily reflects parasympathetic (vagal) activity and respiratory sinus arrhythmia.

LF/HF Ratio

The ratio of LF to HF power. Used as an indicator of sympathovagal balance.

ANS (Autonomic Nervous System)

The part of the nervous system that regulates involuntary functions including heart rate. Consists of sympathetic and parasympathetic branches.

SNS (Sympathetic Nervous System)

"Fight or Flight" system. Increases heart rate and reduces HRV when activated.

PNS (Parasympathetic Nervous System)

"Rest and Digest" system. Decreases heart rate and increases HRV when dominant.

Poincaré Plot

A scatter plot of consecutive RR intervals (RRn vs RRn+1). The shape of the plot reveals HRV patterns.

SD1

Standard deviation perpendicular to the line of identity in Poincaré plot. Measures short-term variability.

SD2

Standard deviation along the line of identity in Poincaré plot. Measures long-term variability.

R-peak

The highest point in the QRS complex of an ECG waveform, used as a reference for measuring RR intervals.
